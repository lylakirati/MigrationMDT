Poverty and Possibility

Economic motivations of Central American migration to the US

Grace Huang, Lyla Kiratiwudhikul, and Nerine Uyanik

Interactive Data Visualization & Society

Since 1980, more than 3.8 million people have migrated from Central America to live in the United States, and over 86% of them were born in one of the Northern Triangle countries (El Salvador, Guatemala, and Honduras) [1]. As of 2019, an additional estimated 2.1 million irregular (unauthorized) immigrants from Central American countries reside in the United States, with approximately 1.96 million from the Northern Triangle Countries. These numbers represent only the fraction of completed migrations among all immigration attempts, many of them unsuccessful.

Central American migrants embark on the treacherous journey in the hopes of securing a better life for themselves or for the families they leave behind. However, the routes they take are filled with danger and uncertainty, from crossing ​​treacherous terrain to facing violence such as robbery and sexual assault.

So what has driven them to leave their home countries and risk the perilous passage to the U.S.?

The World Food Programme surveyed almost 5,000 households from Honduras, Guatemala, and El Salvador, collecting data about their economic and living situations, as well as their perspectives on migration.

Each dot represents 1 surveyed household.

When asked about their intention to move abroad, a large group of them responded preferring to stay in their home country, citing family separation and rootedness as main factors. However, over 43% say they do want to migrate if given a chance to do so.

So what drives these Central Americans to migrate?

Poverty, food insecurity, and lack of economic opportunities for sustainable livelihoods have been cited as the top factors behind migration.

More than 80 percent reported that they want to move abroad in search of employment opportunities. A large number of households also lack money to secure food and other basic needs, which motivates them to find jobs in other countries and send money home.

Among all of the households surveyed, most reported periodically or persistently lacking the money necessary to secure food and other basic living expenses. Toggle between the different buttons to see breakdowns of income insufficiency by country.

Without sufficient income to support the cost of living at home, people have no choice but to leave and to find work elsewhere.

Twelve% of the households indicate that they receive financial support from abroad at least once a month.

The amount of the remittances is US$152 on average and can go up to US$1,000 per shipment.

This is what the average monthly expenditure of households who receive financial support from abroad looks like. They spend approximately US$205 per month.

Housing costs in the Central American countries are less expensive than in the U.S., but food and drinking water cost more, comprising over 35% of Central American households’ monthly expenditure.

Households who receive no financial support from abroad reported spending less across all categories of monthly expenditures. To afford the more fixed expenses such as rent and transportation, households make greater cuts to spending on food and healthcare.

While the amount of remittances received vary by country and by individual households, the additional source of income plays a substantial role in improving the livelihoods of families that receive them.
